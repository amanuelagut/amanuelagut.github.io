Oil Price Shocks and Agricultural Transport Costs in Colombia 2018–2022

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2022
FRED Banco de la República Pearson Correlation OLS Regression Transport Costs
Abstract
This brief tests whether a statistically significant correlation exists between international oil price shocks and agricultural transport costs in Colombia between 2018 and 2022. Using Brent crude oil prices from FRED and Colombian transport cost indices from Banco de la República/DANE, the analysis applies Pearson correlation with significance testing and OLS regression to quantify the oil-to-transport cost pass-through — a key link in the agricultural supply chain cost structure.
1.
Research Question

Does a statistically significant correlation exist between international oil price shocks and agricultural transport costs in Colombia between 2018 and 2022?

Transport costs are the largest non-commodity cost component in Colombian grain distribution. Oil prices are the primary driver of diesel costs, which in turn drive trucking rates. Quantifying this relationship empirically — rather than assuming it — provides the missing link between international energy markets and domestic food prices.

2.
Data & Sources

The analysis combines international oil price data with Colombian transport cost indicators.

The 2018–2022 window captures the oil price collapse of 2020 (Brent below $20/barrel) and the subsequent surge above $120/barrel in 2022, providing substantial variation for regression analysis.

3.
Methodology

The analysis uses correlation and regression methods with formal significance testing.

Step 1 — Pearson correlation with t-test. CORREL function computes the correlation coefficient, followed by a t-test for significance using the formula t = r × √(n-2) / √(1-r²). This tests whether the observed correlation could be due to chance.

Step 2 — OLS regression with diagnostics. LINEST estimates transport_cost = α + β × oil_price + ε. The coefficient β is interpreted as the average transport cost change per $1 change in Brent crude. R², standard errors, and F-statistic are reported.

Step 3 — Lag structure exploration. The regression is re-estimated with oil prices lagged 1–3 months to identify the typical transmission delay from international oil markets to Colombian transport costs.

4.
Key Finding

The oil-transport cost analysis confirms a statistically significant positive relationship.

Key Finding
The Pearson correlation between Brent crude and the Colombian agricultural transport cost index is positive and statistically significant at the 1% level. The OLS regression indicates that each $10/barrel increase in Brent crude is associated with a measurable increase in the transport cost index. The 1-month lag specification produces the best model fit, suggesting rapid transmission from oil markets to domestic transport pricing.

The rapid and significant transmission from oil prices to transport costs confirms that energy market volatility directly affects food supply chain costs in Colombia — a channel that connects geopolitical events (OPEC decisions, the Russia-Ukraine conflict) to domestic food price dynamics.

5.
What I Learned

Formal hypothesis testing on correlation coefficients — computing t-statistics and comparing to critical values — introduced the discipline of distinguishing between 'appears correlated' and 'is statistically significantly correlated.' This distinction is fundamental to all subsequent inferential work.

The oil-transport-food price transmission chain illustrates how supply chain economics connects seemingly distant markets. This multi-link chain thinking informs the 2023 Power BI dashboard design and the 2025 working paper's analytical framework.

Running lag regressions with diagnostics in Excel's Analysis ToolPak built practical experience with regression output interpretation — coefficients, standard errors, R², p-values — that transfers directly to statsmodels in Python.

6.
Limitation
Data Limitation
The transport cost index is a composite measure that includes non-oil cost components (labor, tolls, vehicle maintenance). A cleaner test would use diesel prices directly, but consistent monthly diesel price series for Colombia at the required frequency were not available for the full period.
