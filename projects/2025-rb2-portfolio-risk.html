Commodity Portfolio Risk Modeling in Latin American Agricultural Markets

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2025
FRED UN Comtrade GARCH VaR Portfolio Risk Volatility Modeling
Abstract
This brief applies GARCH-based volatility modeling to construct risk measures for agricultural commodity portfolios in Latin American emerging markets. Using FRED commodity price series, the analysis estimates GARCH(1,1) models for individual commodities, computes Value-at-Risk (VaR) measures, and constructs a minimum-variance portfolio using the estimated conditional covariance matrix. The brief demonstrates how formal financial risk management techniques can be applied to agricultural commodity portfolios in emerging market contexts.
1.
Research Question

How does GARCH-based volatility modeling improve portfolio risk estimates for agricultural commodity investors in Latin American emerging markets?

The rolling standard deviations computed in the 2022 portfolio assume constant volatility — a known misspecification for financial and commodity time series that exhibit volatility clustering. GARCH models capture this clustering, producing time-varying volatility estimates that more accurately reflect actual risk conditions.

2.
Data & Sources

Monthly commodity return series are computed from FRED price data for the major agricultural commodities relevant to Latin American markets.

Variable Source Frequency Period Access
Commodity prices (corn, coffee, soybeans, sugar, wheat)FREDMonthly2015–2025fred.stlouisfed.org

Five commodities are selected for their combined relevance to Colombian and Latin American agricultural trade: corn, coffee, soybeans, sugar, and wheat. Monthly returns are computed as log differences of price levels.

3.
Methodology

The analysis applies formal financial econometrics to agricultural commodity risk measurement.

Step 1 — Return series computation and diagnostics. Log returns computed from price levels, with Jarque-Bera tests for normality, Ljung-Box tests for serial correlation in returns and squared returns (the latter testing for ARCH effects).

Step 2 — GARCH(1,1) estimation. For each commodity, a GARCH(1,1) model is estimated using Python's arch library, with maximum likelihood estimation and diagnostic checks on standardized residuals.

Step 3 — Value-at-Risk computation. 1% and 5% VaR computed from the GARCH conditional variance, compared against historical VaR and constant-variance VaR to demonstrate the improvement from conditional modeling.

Step 4 — Minimum-variance portfolio. The estimated conditional covariance matrix is used to construct a minimum-variance portfolio, with weights compared against naive (equal-weight) and trade-weight-based allocations.

4.
Key Finding

The GARCH-based risk modeling produces significantly different risk estimates than constant-variance approaches.

Key Finding
All five commodity return series exhibit statistically significant ARCH effects (p < 0.01 on Ljung-Box tests of squared returns), validating the use of GARCH modeling. GARCH-based VaR estimates exceed constant-variance VaR by 20–40% during high-volatility regimes (2020, 2022) while being similar during tranquil periods, demonstrating that constant-variance models systematically underestimate tail risk. The minimum-variance portfolio reduces portfolio volatility by approximately 25% relative to equal weighting, with coffee and sugar receiving the highest weights due to their lower covariance with grain prices.

The finding that constant-variance models underestimate risk during crises has direct practical implications: agricultural commodity traders and importers who use static risk measures are underhedged precisely when hedging matters most. GARCH-based risk management adapts to current market conditions.

5.
What I Learned

GARCH modeling formalized the volatility dynamics that the 2020 coefficient of variation could only hint at. The mathematical framework — conditional variance as a function of past shocks and past variance — captures the empirical reality that agricultural prices exhibit 'volatility of volatility.'

Value-at-Risk computation connected academic time-series econometrics to practical financial risk management. VaR is the language that banks, commodity traders, and risk managers use — translating GARCH output into VaR makes the research actionable.

Portfolio optimization with conditional covariance demonstrated that diversification benefits in agricultural commodities are time-varying — correlations increase during crises, reducing the diversification benefit precisely when it is most needed. This has implications for hedging strategy design.

6.
Limitation
Method Limitation
The GARCH(1,1) specification, while standard, does not capture asymmetric volatility responses (leverage effects) or long-memory processes. Extensions like EGARCH or FIGARCH could improve the model but add complexity. Additionally, the portfolio optimization assumes constant expected returns, which is a strong assumption for trending commodity prices.
