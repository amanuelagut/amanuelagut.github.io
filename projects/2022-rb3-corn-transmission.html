Chicago-to-Colombia: Corn Price Transmission Analysis 2018–2022

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2022
FRED SIPSA/DANE Time Series Lag Analysis Price Transmission CBOT
Abstract
This brief models the transmission of corn price shocks from the Chicago Board of Trade (CBOT) to Colombian wholesale markets over 2018–2022. Using FRED international price data and SIPSA-DANE domestic series, the analysis applies time-series regression with multiple lag specifications to estimate the speed, magnitude, and completeness of international-to-domestic price pass-through for corn — Colombia's largest grain import by volume.
1.
Research Question

How do price shocks originating at the Chicago Board of Trade transmit to Colombian wholesale corn markets, and with what lag and magnitude?

Colombia imports over 80% of its corn consumption, primarily from the US. Understanding how CBOT price changes translate to Corabastos wholesale prices — including the delay, the dampening, and the exchange rate amplification — is essential for any corn-dependent supply chain manager or policy analyst.

2.
Data & Sources

The analysis pairs the international benchmark with the Colombian domestic price, using the exchange rate as a transmission channel variable.

The 2018–2022 window includes substantial price variation: the COVID-19 demand collapse (2020), the post-pandemic recovery surge (2021), and the Ukraine-conflict grain price spike (2022). This range provides excellent conditions for transmission analysis.

3.
Methodology

The analysis builds a multi-lag regression framework to estimate transmission speed and magnitude.

Step 1 — Price co-movement visualization. Time-series charts overlay CBOT corn prices (converted to COP using the exchange rate) with SIPSA domestic prices, visually identifying lead-lag patterns and divergence episodes.

Step 2 — Lag regression battery. Regression models are estimated with the domestic price as dependent variable and CBOT price at lags 0, 1, 2, and 3 months as independent variable (plus the exchange rate as control). The lag specification with the highest R² and lowest residual autocorrelation identifies the typical transmission speed.

Step 3 — Pass-through elasticity. The regression coefficient on the international price (in the best-fit lag specification) is interpreted as the pass-through elasticity: a 1% change in CBOT prices produces a β% change in domestic prices. Values below 1 indicate incomplete pass-through.

4.
Key Finding

The corn price transmission analysis reveals a systematic but incomplete pass-through from international to domestic markets.

Key Finding
CBOT corn price changes transmit to Colombian wholesale markets with a 1–2 month lag and approximately 60–70% pass-through elasticity. The exchange rate amplifies the transmission: including USD/COP as a control increases the model's explanatory power significantly. During the 2022 Ukraine-conflict price spike, transmission was faster (near-simultaneous) and more complete (approaching 90% pass-through), suggesting that extreme shocks overcome the buffering mechanisms that normally dampen transmission.

The finding that pass-through increases during extreme events implies that corn market participants in Colombia face asymmetric risk: normal price fluctuations are partially buffered, but tail-risk events transmit almost fully. This has direct implications for hedging strategy design.

5.
What I Learned

Multi-lag regression comparison is the core time-series skill of this project. The systematic evaluation of lag specifications — rather than assuming contemporaneous effects — is a discipline that carries directly into the ARIMA modeling of 2024 and the panel regression of 2025.

The concept of pass-through elasticity — measuring not just direction but degree of price transmission — introduced a quantitative framework for understanding supply chain pricing power that has both academic and practical applications.

The observation that extreme events change transmission dynamics introduced me to the concept of non-linear or regime-dependent behavior in economic relationships — a phenomenon formally captured by the GARCH models implemented in the 2025 portfolio.

6.
Limitation
Method Limitation
The OLS framework does not formally test for cointegration between international and domestic prices — a necessary condition for valid inference on non-stationary time series. The 2024 ARIMA work introduces stationarity testing, and the 2025 working paper applies formal cointegration analysis to this same question.
