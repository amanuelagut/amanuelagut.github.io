Research Brief 3 2023 · 2019 – 2023 · Power BI

Colombia's Agricultural Trade Geography: Key Partners and Flow Patterns 2019–2023

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2023
UN Comtrade DANE Power BI Maps Treemap Trade Geography
Abstract
This brief applies Power BI's geographic mapping and hierarchical visualization tools to explore the spatial distribution and evolution of Colombia's agricultural trade partnerships between 2019 and 2023. Using UN Comtrade bilateral trade data, the analysis creates interactive maps, treemaps, and flow diagrams that reveal partner concentration, regional dependencies, and structural shifts in Colombia's agricultural trade network.
1.
Research Question

How can geographic mapping in Power BI reveal the spatial distribution and evolution of Colombia's agricultural trade partnerships between 2019 and 2023?

The 2021 SQL-based trade network analysis identified structural patterns in numerical form. This brief asks whether geographic visualization can make those patterns more intuitive and reveal spatial dimensions (proximity effects, regional clustering) that tabular analysis misses.

2.
Data & Sources

Bilateral trade data is sourced from UN Comtrade, supplemented with DANE's external trade statistics for Colombian-specific detail.

All bilateral agricultural trade relationships where Colombia is either reporter or partner are included, covering both exports and imports. The analysis separates traditional commodities (coffee, flowers, bananas) from non-traditional agricultural exports.

3.
Methodology

The brief uses Power BI's geographic and hierarchical visualization tools to explore trade network structure.

Step 1 — Bubble map of trade partners. A world map with bubbles sized by trade value shows the geographic distribution of Colombia's agricultural trade network, with color encoding for export vs. import relationships.

Step 2 — Treemap of product-partner composition. Hierarchical treemaps show the product composition of trade with each major partner, revealing whether relationships are diversified or concentrated in a single product.

Step 3 — Year-over-year flow animation. Power BI's play axis feature animates the trade map over time, showing how the geographic distribution of trade shifted between 2019 and 2023.

4.
Key Finding

The geographic visualization reveals that Colombia's agricultural trade network has a strongly asymmetric spatial structure.

Key Finding
Colombia's agricultural exports are geographically concentrated in the Northern Hemisphere (US, EU, Japan) while imports are sourced primarily from Southern Cone countries (Argentina, Brazil) and the US. The treemap analysis shows that export relationships are product-specific (coffee to the US/EU, flowers to the US, bananas to the EU) while import relationships are more diversified by product. The temporal animation reveals a gradual eastward shift in export destinations, with Asian markets growing faster than traditional partners.

The North-South asymmetry in Colombia's trade geography creates differential exposure to geopolitical risks: export revenue depends on Northern Hemisphere demand conditions, while import costs depend on Southern Hemisphere supply conditions and exchange rates.

5.
What I Learned

Geographic visualization as hypothesis generation — the map format revealed spatial clustering patterns (Asian market growth, intra-Andean trade intensification) that were not obvious in the 2021 tabular analysis.

Building treemaps for hierarchical data taught me to use visual hierarchy as an analytical tool — showing the product composition within each trade relationship simultaneously rather than requiring separate queries.

The animated play axis for temporal evolution introduced the concept of data storytelling through time — showing change as a narrative rather than a static comparison.

6.
Limitation
Tool Limitation
Power BI maps use Bing Maps geocoding, which occasionally misplaces small trading partners or aggregates small island states incorrectly. Additionally, the bubble sizing algorithm can make small trade relationships invisible when dominant partners are included. The 2025 working paper uses formal gravity model estimation rather than visual exploration to quantify geographic trade patterns.
