Structural Determinants of Colombian Agricultural Export Competitiveness 2015–2025

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2025
UN Comtrade World Bank RCA IV Regression Export Competitiveness Logistics
Abstract
This brief applies Revealed Comparative Advantage (RCA) analysis and instrumental variable regression to identify the structural determinants of Colombian agricultural export competitiveness over 2015–2025. Using UN Comtrade trade data, World Bank Logistics Performance Index, and exchange rate data, the analysis estimates which factors — logistics quality, exchange rate, productivity — most strongly determine Colombia's competitive position in agricultural export markets relative to regional and global peers.
1.
Research Question

What structural factors — logistics quality, exchange rate, productivity — most strongly determine Colombia's agricultural export competitiveness between 2015 and 2025?

The 2021 SQL analysis mapped Colombia's trade network structure; the 2023 Power BI dashboard visualized trade geography. This brief asks the causal question: what structural factors explain why Colombia's agricultural exports are competitive in some products and markets but not others?

2.

The analysis combines trade flow data with structural determinant indicators from multiple sources.

RCA indices are computed for each Colombian agricultural product using the standard Balassa formula. The regression sample includes 10 Latin American countries over 10 years, with product-level RCA as the dependent variable.

3.
Methodology

The analysis combines trade competitiveness measurement with formal causal inference techniques.

Step 1 — Revealed Comparative Advantage computation. Balassa RCA indices computed for each country-product-year combination: RCA = (X_ij / X_i) / (X_wj / X_w), where values above 1 indicate revealed comparative advantage.

Step 2 — Panel regression of RCA determinants. Fixed effects regression estimates RCA_ijt = α + β₁ × Logistics_it + β₂ × REER_it + β₃ × Productivity_it + μ_i + λ_t + ε_ijt, identifying which structural factors predict competitiveness.

Step 3 — Instrumental variable estimation. To address potential endogeneity (competitive countries may invest more in logistics), an IV strategy uses geographic distance to major ports as an instrument for logistics quality.

4.
Key Finding

The competitiveness analysis identifies logistics quality as the most policy-relevant determinant.

Key Finding
Logistics quality (World Bank LPI) is the strongest and most robust predictor of agricultural export competitiveness across Latin American countries. A one-standard-deviation improvement in LPI is associated with a significant increase in average RCA. Exchange rate effects are significant but smaller in magnitude, and agricultural productivity shows the expected positive sign but is less robust across specifications. The IV estimates suggest that the OLS results, if anything, underestimate the causal effect of logistics quality.

The finding that logistics infrastructure matters more than exchange rate or productivity for export competitiveness has clear policy implications: Colombia's agricultural export potential is constrained more by infrastructure bottlenecks than by macroeconomic conditions or farm-level productivity. This aligns with the author's operational experience in grain distribution, where transport and logistics costs were consistently identified as the binding constraint.

5.
What I Learned

Revealed Comparative Advantage analysis connects trade theory (Ricardian comparative advantage) to empirical measurement, providing a bridge between abstract economic concepts and measurable trade outcomes.

Instrumental variable estimation addresses the fundamental challenge of causal inference in observational data. The discipline of finding valid instruments and testing exclusion restrictions represents the highest level of econometric rigor in the portfolio.

The convergence of empirical findings with operational experience — that logistics constraints matter most — validates the research agenda's grounding in real-world observation. The six-year journey from warehouse observation to formal econometric evidence closes a full analytical loop.

6.
Limitation
Data & Identification Limitation
The World Bank LPI is only available biennially, requiring interpolation for annual panel construction. The geographic instrument for logistics quality may violate the exclusion restriction if coastal proximity directly affects agricultural trade patterns (e.g., through access to maritime shipping). Alternative instruments (historical road network density) would strengthen the identification strategy but are not available for all countries in the sample.
