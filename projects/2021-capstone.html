Latin American Agricultural Trade Networks 2015–2021: A SQL-Based Multi-Source Analysis

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2021
UN Comtrade SQL Multi-table JOIN Trade Networks Latin America
Abstract
This capstone applies structured SQL queries to UN Comtrade trade data to identify structural patterns in Latin American agricultural trade networks over 2015–2021. Using multi-table joins, subqueries, and aggregation functions, the analysis crosses bilateral trade flows, product classifications, and partner country data to reveal concentration patterns, partner dependencies, and shifts in trade composition. The transition from spreadsheet-based to database-driven analysis marks a methodological step change in the portfolio, enabling analysis at a scale not feasible with manual data manipulation.
1.
Research Question

What structural patterns emerge in Latin American agricultural trade networks when multiple UN Comtrade tables are queried and crossed using SQL?

The 2020 portfolio demonstrated that Colombian agricultural markets are sensitive to external shocks. Understanding the trade network structure — who trades what with whom, and how concentrated those flows are — provides the structural context for interpreting price dynamics. SQL enables this analysis at a bilateral, product-level granularity that spreadsheet tools cannot efficiently handle.

2.
Data & Sources

All trade data is sourced from UN Comtrade, the most comprehensive publicly available bilateral trade database, using HS (Harmonized System) commodity codes for agricultural products.

Agricultural commodities are defined using HS chapters 01–24 (live animals, vegetable products, fats, prepared foodstuffs). The geographic scope covers 10 Latin American countries with significant agricultural trade: Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, Mexico, Paraguay, Peru, Uruguay, and Bolivia.

3.
Methodology

The analysis progresses from simple queries to complex multi-table operations, reflecting the SQL skill progression over the year.

Step 1 — Single-table aggregation. Basic SELECT/GROUP BY queries compute total export and import values by country and year, establishing the top-level trade landscape for each country.

Step 2 — Multi-table JOIN. Trade flow tables are joined with commodity classification tables to identify which agricultural products dominate each country's export and import portfolio. LEFT JOINs handle cases where product codes appear in flows but not in classification metadata.

Step 3 — Partner concentration analysis. Subqueries compute Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) equivalents for each country's trade partner distribution, identifying which countries have diversified vs. concentrated trade networks.

Step 4 — Temporal shift detection. Year-over-year growth rates computed via self-joins identify which products and partners gained or lost share over the period, revealing structural shifts in the regional trade architecture.

4.
Key Finding

The SQL-based analysis reveals significant heterogeneity in trade network structure across Latin American agricultural exporters.

Key Finding
Colombia's agricultural export network is more concentrated by partner (higher HHI) than Brazil's or Argentina's, with the United States and European Union accounting for a disproportionate share of coffee and flower exports. Intra-regional agricultural trade (within Latin America) grew faster than extra-regional trade over 2015–2021, driven by grain flows between Argentina/Brazil and Andean importing countries. The most rapidly growing product categories were oilseeds and processed food products.

The concentration of Colombia's trade network in a few partners and products creates structural vulnerability — a disruption in any single relationship (e.g., a tariff change, a phytosanitary ban) would have disproportionate impact compared to more diversified exporters like Brazil.

5.
What I Learned

The transition from spreadsheet to database thinking was the central skill of this year. SQL's declarative logic — specifying what you want rather than how to compute it step by step — fundamentally changed how I approach data analysis. The same cross-tabulation that required hours of manual pivot table work in 2020 can be expressed in a single JOIN query.

Writing subqueries to compute derived metrics (like HHI concentration indices) within a larger query taught me the power of composable analysis — building complex results from simpler building blocks. This compositional logic translates directly to function-based Python programming in 2024.

Self-joins for temporal comparison (joining a table to itself at t and t-1) introduced a technique for computing growth rates and detecting structural changes without external tools — a pattern that recurs in panel data construction in 2025.

6.
Limitation
Data Limitation
UN Comtrade data has known reporting asymmetries (exporter and importer reports for the same flow often differ). The analysis uses reporter data without mirror analysis. Additionally, HS classification changes between 2012 and 2017 create potential inconsistencies in product-level time series. A more rigorous approach would apply concordance tables to harmonize classification changes.
7.
Deliverable

The deliverable is a .sql script file containing all queries (annotated with comments), plus an Excel workbook with exported query results formatted as summary tables and charts.
