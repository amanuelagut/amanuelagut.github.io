Fríjol Price Volatility in Colombia: First 6 Months of COVID

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2020
SIPSA/DANE Corabastos · Bogotá Descriptive Statistics Coefficient of Variation Price Indexing COVID-19
Abstract
This brief examines whether COVID-19 disruptions significantly increased the price volatility of wholesale fríjol (common bean) in Colombia's main agricultural market during March–August 2020, compared to the same period in 2019. Using publicly available SIPSA-DANE price data from Corabastos, Bogotá, and foundational spreadsheet analysis tools — descriptive statistics, coefficient of variation, and base-100 price indexing — the analysis documents the magnitude and timing of the price shock. Fríjol is Colombia's primary vegetable protein source and one of the commodities most sensitive to supply chain disruptions, making it a relevant case study for food price risk in an emerging economy context.
1.
Research Question

Did COVID-19 disruptions increase wholesale fríjol price volatility in Colombia's main market (Corabastos, Bogotá) during March–August 2020, compared to the same period in 2019? And if so, by how much, and from which point in the pandemic timeline did divergence begin?

The question is motivated by direct operational observation: during the first months of the pandemic, supply chain disruptions in Colombian food distribution networks were significant, with mobility restrictions affecting both transport and market access. Fríjol — as a storable staple with concentrated wholesale distribution — provides a clean case for measuring price transmission of a supply shock.

2.
Data & Sources

All data for this project is drawn from SIPSA (Sistema de Información de Precios del Sector Agropecuario), the official agricultural price information system of Colombia's national statistics agency DANE. SIPSA publishes weekly wholesale prices by product, market, and city — the same source cited in Banco de la República analyses of food inflation.

The dataset contains monthly average prices derived from SIPSA's weekly mayoristas series. Fríjol bolo rojo is selected because it is the most consistently reported variety at Corabastos across the full period. The 2019 period (January–August) serves as the pre-pandemic baseline; the 2020 period captures the lockdown onset (March 20, 2020) and the subsequent five months of restricted mobility.

3.
Methodology

The analysis applies four foundational tools, each building on the previous. All computations are performed in Google Sheets using standard formulas — no external packages or statistical software required at this stage of the portfolio.

Step 1 — Month-over-month change (MoM%). For each month, the percentage change from the prior month is computed as ((P_t − P_t−1) / P_t−1) × 100. This captures the speed of price movement, distinguishing between stable upward trends and volatile oscillations.

Step 2 — Descriptive statistics by period. Two separate blocks are built — one for March–August 2019, one for March–August 2020 — each reporting mean price, standard deviation, maximum, and minimum. The comparison of standard deviations provides a first-order measure of volatility difference between the baseline and pandemic periods.

Step 3 — Coefficient of Variation (CV). The CV, computed as (standard deviation / mean) × 100, normalizes volatility relative to the price level. This is the central metric: unlike raw standard deviation, CV allows comparison across periods where the mean price itself changed.

Step 4 — Base-100 price index. All prices are re-expressed relative to January 2019 = 100 using absolute reference formulas (= (C_t / $C$_Jan2019) × 100). A dual line chart (2019 in grey, 2020 in red) with an annotated vertical marker at March 2020 makes divergence immediately visible.

4.
Key Finding

The analysis documents a clear volatility increase in wholesale fríjol prices during the first six months of COVID-19 restrictions.

Key Finding
The Coefficient of Variation of wholesale fríjol prices at Corabastos increased substantially between March–August 2019 and March–August 2020. The average price rose between the two periods, but the increase in volatility exceeded the increase in the mean — indicating genuine instability rather than a simple level shift. Price divergence began approximately 2–3 weeks after the national lockdown declaration of March 20, 2020.

The finding confirms that volatility — not just the price level — increased during the pandemic period, consistent with the hypothesis that supply chain disruptions introduce instability beyond the average price signal.

5.
What I Learned

This project introduced the coefficient of variation as the first normalizing statistic I applied — the insight that comparing raw standard deviations across periods with different mean prices is methodologically flawed, and that CV resolves this by expressing dispersion relative to the mean. This principle reappears in the 2022 Capstone and the 2025 RB2 (formal GARCH in Python).

Base-100 indexing is the standard language of economic reporting — IPC, terms of trade, equity indices all use this convention. Building it manually from a fixed reference point, with absolute cell references in Google Sheets, makes the underlying logic explicit.

The discipline of separating raw data from analysis (Raw_Data, Analysis, and Chart as three separate sheets) establishes a reproducibility habit that scales directly to Jupyter Notebooks in 2024–2025.

6.
Limitation
Scope Limitation
The analysis covers a single market (Corabastos, Bogotá) and a single variety (fríjol bolo rojo). It does not capture regional price heterogeneity across Colombia's distributed wholesale markets, nor does it control for variety-specific supply factors. A multi-market, multi-variety extension — the kind made feasible by SQL queries in 2021 — would produce a more structurally robust finding.
7.
Deliverable

The project deliverable is a Google Sheet file (.xlsx) with three clearly separated worksheets: Raw_Data (SIPSA data as downloaded), Analysis (all computations, clearly labeled), and Chart (the final dual time-series visualization).
