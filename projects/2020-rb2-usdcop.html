Research Brief 2 2020 · January 2018 – December 2020 · Google Sheets · Excel

USD/COP Exchange Rate and Grain Import Costs in Colombia 2018–2020

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2020
FRED Banco de la República Correlation OLS Regression Exchange Rate COVID-19
Abstract
This brief investigates the relationship between the USD/COP exchange rate and grain import costs in Colombia between 2018 and 2020, with particular attention to how this relationship changed during the COVID-19 shock. Using FRED exchange rate data and Banco de la República trade statistics, the analysis applies Pearson correlation and simple OLS regression in Google Sheets to quantify the pass-through from currency depreciation to food import costs. The Colombian peso experienced one of its sharpest depreciations in March 2020, making this period a natural experiment for exchange rate transmission analysis.
1.
Research Question

What is the relationship between the USD/COP exchange rate and grain import costs in Colombia, and how did it change during the COVID-19 shock?

Colombia imports a significant share of its corn and wheat consumption. When the peso depreciates, the COP-denominated cost of these imports rises — but the speed and completeness of this pass-through is an empirical question. The March 2020 depreciation (from ~3,400 to ~4,100 COP/USD in weeks) provides a stress test for measuring transmission speed.

2.

The analysis combines international financial data with Colombian trade and price statistics.

The USD/COP series is the Federal Reserve's daily noon rate averaged to monthly frequency. Import cost data is drawn from Banco de la República's external sector statistics. The three-year window captures a stable pre-COVID period (2018–Feb 2020) and the full shock-and-recovery cycle (Mar–Dec 2020).

3.
Methodology

The analysis uses three techniques, progressing from descriptive to inferential.

Step 1 — Scatter plot and Pearson correlation. The USD/COP rate is plotted against grain import costs to visualize the relationship. Pearson's r is computed using the CORREL function in Google Sheets, separately for the pre-COVID and COVID periods, to test whether the correlation strengthened or weakened under stress.

Step 2 — Simple OLS regression. Using LINEST, a linear regression estimates import cost = α + β × (USD/COP) + ε. The coefficient β measures the average COP import cost increase per unit of exchange rate depreciation. The regression is run on the full sample and on pre/post-COVID subsamples.

Step 3 — Lag analysis. To test whether exchange rate changes transmit with a delay, the regression is re-run with 1-month and 2-month lagged exchange rate values. The lag structure that produces the highest R² suggests the typical transmission speed.

4.
Key Finding

The exchange rate analysis reveals a significant but incomplete pass-through from currency depreciation to grain import costs.

Key Finding
The Pearson correlation between USD/COP and grain import costs was positive and significant across the full sample period. The COVID-period subsample showed a temporarily stronger correlation as the sharp depreciation coincided with import cost spikes. The 1-month lagged specification produced the best fit, suggesting that exchange rate shocks transmit to import costs with approximately a one-month delay in Colombian grain markets.

The incomplete pass-through implies that importers partially absorb exchange rate shocks through margin compression or inventory buffers — a finding consistent with the operational experience of grain distributors who maintain strategic stock levels precisely to hedge against short-term currency movements.

5.
What I Learned

This project introduced lag structure analysis — the idea that economic relationships operate with temporal delays, and that testing different lag specifications is a standard empirical practice. This concept becomes central in the 2022 corn price transmission study and the 2024 ARIMA forecasting work.

Computing Pearson correlation on subsamples taught me that a correlation coefficient is not a fixed property of two variables but depends on the sample period — a foundational insight for understanding structural breaks and regime changes in time-series analysis.

The practical experience of downloading FRED data and aligning it with Colombian sources in different frequencies (daily vs. weekly vs. monthly) introduced the data harmonization challenges that become routine in 2024–2025 Python workflows.

6.
Limitation
Method Limitation
The OLS regression does not account for non-stationarity in the exchange rate series or potential endogeneity between exchange rates and import volumes. A proper cointegration analysis — introduced conceptually in the 2022 portfolio and implemented in Python in 2025 — would provide a more rigorous test of the long-run equilibrium relationship.
