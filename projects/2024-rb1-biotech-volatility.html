Research Brief 1 2024 · 2010 – 2024 · Python — pandas, matplotlib

Biotech Seed Adoption and Agricultural Price Volatility in Latin America 2010–2024

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2024
FAO ISAAA pandas matplotlib Biotechnology Volatility
Abstract
This brief tests whether higher biotech seed adoption correlates with lower agricultural price volatility across Latin American countries between 2010 and 2024. Using FAO price and production data merged with ISAAA biotechnology adoption statistics in Python (pandas), the analysis computes country-level volatility measures and explores their cross-sectional relationship with biotech adoption rates, building the empirical foundation for the formal panel regression conducted in the 2025 portfolio.
1.
Research Question

Does higher biotech seed adoption correlate with lower agricultural price volatility across Latin American countries between 2010 and 2024?

One theoretical benefit of biotech seeds is yield stabilization — reduced crop loss from pests and weather shocks should reduce supply volatility and, consequently, price volatility. This brief provides the first empirical test of this hypothesis using Latin American data.

2.
Data & Sources

The analysis merges agricultural production data with biotechnology adoption metrics at the country-year level.

Variable Source Frequency Period Access
Agricultural producer pricesFAO FAOSTATAnnual2010–2024fao.org/faostat
Biotech crop area by countryISAAAAnnual2010–2024isaaa.org
Crop production volumesFAO FAOSTATAnnual2010–2024fao.org/faostat

Eight Latin American countries with available biotechnology data are included. Price volatility is computed as the coefficient of variation over rolling 5-year windows for each country-crop combination.

3.
Methodology

The analysis uses pandas for data merging and computation, matplotlib for visualization, and cross-sectional correlation for initial hypothesis testing.

Step 1 — Data merging in pandas. FAO and ISAAA datasets are merged on country-year keys using pd.merge(), with careful handling of different country naming conventions between sources.

Step 2 — Rolling volatility computation. 5-year rolling CVs computed using pandas .rolling() method for each country-crop price series, producing a panel of volatility observations.

Step 3 — Cross-sectional correlation. For each year, the correlation between biotech adoption rates and price volatility across countries is computed, creating a time series of cross-sectional correlations.

4.
Key Finding

The cross-sectional analysis reveals a suggestive but not conclusive negative relationship between biotech adoption and price volatility.

Key Finding
Countries with higher biotech adoption rates tend to show lower price volatility for the crops where biotech varieties are available (corn, soybeans). However, the relationship is not statistically robust in simple cross-sectional analysis due to the small number of countries and the confounding influence of market size, trade openness, and policy interventions. The correlation is stronger for soybeans (where biotech penetration is highest) than for corn.

The suggestive pattern motivates formal econometric analysis that can control for confounders. The 2025 RB1 addresses this with panel regression including country and time fixed effects.

5.
What I Learned

Data merging in pandas — using pd.merge() with different join types and handling naming inconsistencies — is the Python equivalent of SQL JOINs learned in 2021, but with more flexible data structures and in-memory processing.

The rolling window approach to volatility using .rolling() extends the static CV computation from 2020 into a dynamic, time-varying measure that better captures how volatility evolves.

The experience of finding a suggestive but inconclusive result taught the importance of analytical honesty — reporting what the data shows rather than what the hypothesis predicts, and identifying the analytical improvements needed for a stronger test.

6.
Limitation
Method Limitation
Cross-sectional correlation with 8 observations provides low statistical power and cannot control for confounders. Panel regression with fixed effects (implemented in 2025) is the appropriate method for this question. The brief serves as exploratory analysis rather than a definitive test.
