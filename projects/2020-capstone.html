Colombian Grain Price Dynamics During COVID-19: A Multi-Market Analysis

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2020
SIPSA-DANE FRED Banco de la República Multiple Regression Dashboard Correlation Matrix COVID-19
Abstract
This capstone integrates three parallel research briefs into a unified multi-market analysis of Colombian grain price dynamics during the COVID-19 pandemic (2018–2020). Using SIPSA-DANE wholesale price data across multiple products and cities, combined with FRED exchange rate series and Banco de la República macroeconomic indicators, the analysis applies descriptive statistics, correlation matrices, regression modeling, and base-100 price indexing within Google Sheets. The project documents how a systemic supply chain shock — national lockdowns, mobility restrictions, and logistics disruptions — transmitted unevenly across grain markets, producing heterogeneous volatility patterns that simple national averages obscure.
1.
Research Question

What systematic price patterns emerge when crossing products, cities, and time periods across Colombia's main grain wholesale markets during the COVID-19 shock?

The COVID-19 pandemic created a natural experiment in supply chain disruption. Colombia's centralized wholesale market structure — with Corabastos in Bogotá handling a disproportionate share of national grain flows — meant that lockdown-induced bottlenecks would manifest clearly in price data. This capstone tests whether the disruption was uniform or whether product-specific and region-specific factors created divergent price trajectories.

Three grain products — fríjol bolo rojo, arroz paddy, and maíz amarillo — were selected based on consistent SIPSA reporting across both Bogotá (Corabastos) and Ibagué (Plaza de la 21). The 2018–2019 window provides 24 months of pre-pandemic baseline; the 2020 window captures the full lockdown and partial reopening cycle.

3.
Methodology

The capstone applies four analytical layers, each building on the skills developed in the three research briefs conducted during 2020.

Step 1 — Multi-product descriptive statistics. Mean, standard deviation, coefficient of variation, and min/max computed for each product × city × period combination. This produces a 3×2×2 matrix (3 products, 2 cities, 2 periods: pre-COVID vs. COVID) that makes cross-market comparison systematic rather than anecdotal.

Step 2 — Correlation matrix. Pearson correlation coefficients computed across all product-city price pairs and the USD/COP exchange rate. The matrix identifies which markets move together (high integration) and which diverge under stress (segmentation).

Step 3 — Multiple regression. Using LINEST in Google Sheets, a regression model estimates the relationship between grain wholesale prices (dependent) and the exchange rate, oil prices, and a COVID dummy variable (independent). The model tests whether the pandemic effect is statistically distinguishable from exchange rate and oil price movements.

Step 4 — Base-100 indexed time series with dashboard. All price series re-expressed relative to January 2018 = 100, plotted as overlapping time series with an annotated vertical marker at March 2020. A Google Sheets dashboard with drop-down filters for product and city allows interactive exploration.

4.
Key Finding

The multi-market analysis reveals that the COVID-19 shock did not affect all grain markets equally — a finding that would be invisible in aggregate national price statistics.

Key Finding
Fríjol prices at Corabastos showed the highest COVID-period volatility increase (CV rising from approximately 8% to over 18%), while rice prices remained comparatively stable (CV change of ~2 percentage points). The USD/COP exchange rate explains a larger share of price variation in imported grains (corn) than in domestically produced staples (fríjol). Regional divergence between Bogotá and Ibagué was most pronounced in April–June 2020, coinciding with peak mobility restrictions.

These results suggest that food price risk in Colombia during supply chain disruptions is highly product-specific and geography-dependent, reinforcing the case for disaggregated monitoring systems rather than national-level food price indices alone.

5.
What I Learned

The capstone taught me how to integrate multiple analytical threads into a coherent narrative. Each research brief (fríjol volatility, exchange rate effects, regional comparison) addresses one dimension; the capstone shows that these dimensions interact — that volatility, exchange rate transmission, and geographic segmentation are not independent phenomena but facets of the same market disruption.

Building a Google Sheets dashboard with drop-down filters — using Data Validation, INDIRECT references, and conditional chart ranges — introduced me to the logic of interactive data exploration that I would later implement in Power BI (2023) and Jupyter Notebooks (2024). The constraint of working within spreadsheet formulas forced me to understand the underlying logic before using dedicated tools.

The experience of writing multiple regression in LINEST within a spreadsheet — manually constructing the design matrix, interpreting coefficient arrays, and computing R² — built intuition for what statsmodels and sklearn automate in Python. When I reached Python in 2024, the econometric concepts were already internalized.

6.
Limitation
Scope & Data Limitation
The analysis is limited to three grain products and two cities. A more comprehensive study would include perishable products (fruits, vegetables), additional regional markets, and weekly rather than monthly frequency to capture intra-month volatility spikes. The regression model uses OLS without testing for heteroscedasticity or autocorrelation — issues addressed in the 2022 Capstone with formal time-series diagnostics.
7.
Deliverable

The deliverable is a multi-sheet Google Sheets workbook (.xlsx export) containing: Raw_Data (three SIPSA series + FRED exchange rate), Analysis (descriptive statistics, correlation matrix, regression output), Dashboard (interactive chart with product/city filters), and a Summary sheet with key findings. All formulas are preserved and labeled.
