Price Determinants of Rice Wholesale Markets in Colombia 2019–2022

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2022
SIPSA/DANE USDA Multiple Regression Rice Markets Price Determinants
Abstract
This brief applies multiple regression analysis to identify the key determinants of wholesale rice prices across Colombian markets between 2019 and 2022. Using SIPSA-DANE domestic price data, FRED exchange rates, and international rice price benchmarks from USDA, the analysis tests which factors — exchange rate, oil prices, seasonality — best explain observed price variation. Rice is Colombia's most widely consumed grain and has a partially domestic, partially imported supply structure that makes it sensitive to both local and international forces.
1.
Research Question

What factors — exchange rate, oil prices, seasonality — best explain wholesale rice price variation across Colombian markets between 2019 and 2022?

Rice pricing in Colombia is influenced by a complex mix of domestic production cycles, import competition, government price supports, and macroeconomic variables. Identifying which factors dominate empirically helps prioritize risk management strategies for market participants.

2.
Data & Sources

The analysis combines domestic price data with international benchmarks and macroeconomic controls.

Monthly observations for 48 months (2019–2022) provide sufficient degrees of freedom for multiple regression with 3–4 independent variables. Seasonal dummy variables (harvest months: March–April, August–September) capture production cycle effects.

3.
Methodology

A stepwise multiple regression approach tests individual and combined explanatory power of each potential determinant.

Step 1 — Univariate correlations. Each potential determinant is correlated individually with rice prices to establish bivariate relationships before multivariate modeling.

Step 2 — Stepwise regression. Variables are added sequentially to the regression model, with each addition tested for incremental explanatory power (change in R²). This identifies which variables contribute independently vs. redundantly.

Step 3 — Full model with diagnostics. The final model includes all significant predictors with full diagnostic output: R², adjusted R², F-statistic, individual t-tests, and residual analysis for heteroscedasticity patterns.

4.
Key Finding

The multiple regression analysis identifies the exchange rate and seasonality as the dominant price determinants for Colombian wholesale rice.

Key Finding
The USD/COP exchange rate and seasonal harvest dummies together explain the majority of rice price variation over the period. International rice price benchmarks add modest incremental explanatory power, suggesting that domestic supply cycles dominate international price transmission for rice — unlike corn, where international prices are the primary driver. Oil prices contribute mainly through their correlation with the exchange rate rather than as an independent factor.

The dominance of domestic factors in rice pricing — contrasting with corn's international dependence — reflects rice's partially self-sufficient supply structure in Colombia. This product-specific difference in price determinant structure is a key insight for differential risk management approaches.

5.
What I Learned

Stepwise regression taught me to think about model building as an incremental process — testing each variable's marginal contribution rather than dumping everything into one model. This principle of parsimony carries through all subsequent econometric work.

The product-specific finding — that rice and corn have fundamentally different price determinant structures — reinforced the lesson from 2020 that aggregate food price analysis obscures product-level heterogeneity.

Constructing seasonal dummy variables in Excel (binary 0/1 columns for harvest months) introduced the concept of categorical encoding in regression, which becomes standard practice in Python's pandas get_dummies() and statsmodels.

6.
Limitation
Method Limitation
The multiple regression assumes a linear, additive relationship between determinants and rice prices. Interaction effects (e.g., the exchange rate effect may be stronger during harvest season) are not tested. Additionally, the 48-month sample size limits the number of variables and lags that can be reliably included without overfitting.
