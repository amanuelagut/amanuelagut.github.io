Colombian Agricultural Export Evolution: Key Trading Partners 2015–2021

Angie Manuela Gutierrez-Oviedo · Fundación Universitaria del Área Andina · 2021
UN Comtrade SQL Export Analysis Trade Partners
Abstract
This brief traces the evolution of Colombian agricultural exports to key trading partners over 2015–2021, identifying which partners gained or lost share. Using SQL queries on UN Comtrade bilateral trade data, the analysis computes annual export values, partner shares, and growth rates for Colombia's top agricultural export destinations. The results reveal a gradually diversifying export portfolio with emerging Asian markets gaining share from traditional North American and European partners.
1.
Research Question

How did Colombian agricultural exports to key trading partners evolve between 2015 and 2021, and which partners gained or lost share?

Colombia's agricultural export profile is historically concentrated in coffee and flowers directed to the US and EU. Understanding whether diversification is occurring — both by product and by destination — is relevant for assessing the country's trade vulnerability and the effectiveness of export promotion policies.

2.
Data & Sources

Bilateral export data is sourced from UN Comtrade, filtered for Colombian agricultural exports (HS chapters 01–24).

Variable Source Frequency Period Access
Colombian agricultural exports (USD)UN ComtradeAnnual2015–2021comtrade.un.org

The top 15 export destinations by cumulative value over the period are selected. Partner shares are computed as each destination's percentage of total Colombian agricultural exports per year.

3.
Methodology

The analysis uses progressively complex SQL queries to extract and compute partner-level export dynamics.

Step 1 — Aggregate exports by partner and year. SELECT partner, year, SUM(value) queries compute annual export totals for each destination country.

Step 2 — Partner share computation. Window functions or subqueries calculate each partner's share of total exports per year, enabling share-based comparison independent of absolute value fluctuations.

Step 3 — Growth rate ranking. Year-over-year growth rates identify which partners are gaining momentum, with compound annual growth rates (CAGR) computed for the full period.

4.
Key Finding

The partner evolution analysis reveals a gradual but measurable diversification of Colombia's agricultural export destinations.

Key Finding
While the United States remained Colombia's largest agricultural export destination throughout the period, its share declined moderately. Asian markets — particularly Japan and South Korea — showed the highest growth rates in Colombian agricultural imports. Intra-regional trade with Ecuador and Peru also expanded, particularly in processed food categories.

The diversification trend reduces Colombia's dependence on any single trading partner, though the pace of change is slow relative to competitors like Peru and Chile, which have pursued more aggressive trade agreement strategies in Asia-Pacific.

5.
What I Learned

Writing SQL window functions for share computation introduced partition-based analysis — computing metrics within groups without collapsing the data. This logic directly informs the panel data structures used in 2025.

The experience of interpreting trade share changes — distinguishing between absolute growth and relative share shifts — built analytical intuition for proportional reasoning that applies across all economic analysis.

Structuring queries for export ranking with ORDER BY and LIMIT clauses taught me to think about data presentation as part of the query design, not just a post-processing step.

6.
Limitation
Scope Limitation
The analysis does not disaggregate by product within each partner relationship. A partner gaining share might be driven entirely by one product (e.g., flowers to Japan) rather than broad-based diversification. The 2025 RB3 addresses this limitation with product × partner cross-tabulations.
